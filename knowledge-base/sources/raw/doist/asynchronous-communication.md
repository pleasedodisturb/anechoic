And what you can do to start implementing it, whether you're an individual contributor or a team leader.

## The Core Insight

Study after study into remote work has clarified that **remote workers are more productive than their office-bound counterparts**. What's not entirely clear is why.

Yes, people gain back time (and sanity) by avoiding rush hour commutes. They avoid the distractions of the office. They regain a sense of control over their workdays.

**But apart from the commute, all of those benefits aren't necessarily the result of location independence, but rather the byproduct of asynchronous communication** — giving employees control over when they communicate with their teammates.

> **Teams who try to go remote without putting in place tools, workflows, and norms for asynchronous communication will fail.**

---

## What is Asynchronous Communication?

**Asynchronous communication is when you send a message without expecting an immediate response.** For example, you send an email. I open and respond to the email several hours later.

**Synchronous – or real-time – communication is when you send a message and the recipient processes the information and responds immediately.** In-person communication, like meetings, are examples of purely synchronous communication.

### Key Insight

> Asynchronous communication isn't primarily about tools — though the right ones help — **it's about team culture and habits.**

Even email is often treated as synchronous communication. A 2015 study found that the most common email response time was just 2 minutes.

| Real-time communication | Asynchronous communication |
|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Communication requires all team members to be present simultaneously, in meetings/video calls or respond quickly to chats/emails | Communication happens in writing or prerecorded video with team members catching up on their own schedules |
| Everyone is tied to more or less the same work schedule | Everyone can work when it works best for them |
| Prioritizes speed of collaboration | Prioritizes focus and flexibility |

## The Problem with Real-Time, All-the-Time Communication

If employees are consistently more productive when working away from the office, **there's something broken about the modern workplace**.

According to Harvard Business Review's "Collaborative Overload", the time employees spend on collaboration has increased by 50% over the past two decades. Workers spend **a full 80%** of their workdays communicating:

- **Email** – workers spend an average of 6 hours a day
- **Meetings** – time spent has ballooned by 253% since the pandemic
- **Instant messaging** – the average Slack user sends 200 messages a day; 1,000-message power users are "not the exception"

> "I used to wake up and turn off the alarm and check Tinder. Now I wake up and check Slack."

### The Core Problems with Synchronous Communication

#### 1. It leads to constant interruptions

Interruptions split people's attention and make it more difficult to make meaningful progress on work. High-value, cognitively-demanding activities — like coding, writing, designing, strategizing, and problem-solving — require long periods of deep, focused work.

**Synchronous communication requires constant context switching** and makes creating large, uninterrupted chunks of time during the workday impossible.

#### 2. It prioritizes being connected over being productive

In real-time environments, you're incentivized to stay connected and available at all times. If you disconnect, discussions will move on before you even have a chance to respond to or even see them.

**To avoid missing out on crucial decisions and discussions, people always try to be online and in as many meetings as possible**, hurting their well-being and productivity.

#### 3. It creates unnecessary stress

The expectation of being constantly available means that workers lack control over their schedules. They spend their workdays attending meetings and responding to requests rather than proactively setting their own agenda.

One study found that people compensate for the time lost to workplace interruptions by attempting to work faster, leading to "more stress, higher frustration, time pressure, and effort". **People often cope by working a "second shift" in the evening when they know they won't be interrupted.**

#### 4. It leads to lower quality discussions and sub-optimal solutions

When you have to respond immediately, people don't have time to think through key issues thoroughly and provide thoughtful responses. **Your first response to any given situation is often not your best response.**

> "Writing is thinking. To write well is to think clearly. That's why it's so hard." — David McCullough

## The Benefits of Asynchronous Communication

Some companies — like GitLab, Zapier, Automattic, Buffer, and Doist — are embracing a more asynchronous approach to collaboration.

### Control over the workday leads to happier and more productive employees

In an async environment, there are no set work hours. Employees have total control over how they structure their workdays to fit their lifestyles, biorhythms, and responsibilities.

Some Doisters work during the night as it suits them the best. The CEO spends an hour with his kids every morning and no one notices.

**Workers want flexible hours *even more* than they want remote work.**

### Async incentivizes higher-quality communication

Async communication is admittedly slower (at least in the immediate sense), but it also tends to be of higher quality:
- People learn to communicate more clearly and thoroughly to avoid unnecessary back-and-forths
- They have time to think through problems and provide more thoughtful responses
- Instead of knee-jerk responses, people can reply when they're ready

**As an added benefit, when people have the time to think through their responses, there tend to be fewer unthinking outbursts. Over the last decade, Doist hasn't had a single serious HR issue.**

### Better planning leads to less stress

Advanced planning is a must when last-minute ASAP requests aren't an option. People learn to plan their workloads and collaborations more carefully to give enough time for coworkers to see and respond to their requests.

### Deep work becomes the default

Because employees don't have to stay on top of each message as it comes in, they can **block off large chunks of uninterrupted time** to do the work that creates the most value for your organization.

They can come back to process their messages in batches 1-3 times a day instead of bouncing back and forth between work and messages or meetings.

### Documentation and greater transparency become automatic

Because most communication happens in writing, **key discussions and important information are documented automatically**. It's easier to share and reference those conversations later.

### Collaboration across time zones is seamless

Communication between time zones becomes smooth, and no one is at an informational disadvantage because of the time zone. That means you're not limiting your hiring pool to specific time zones.

### It puts in-office and remote employees on the same footing

When communication happens asynchronously, in-office and remote employees have access to the same information and conversations. They have an equal opportunity to contribute to discussions and decisions.

### Retention Impact

Doist's employee retention is 90%+ — much higher than the overall tech industry. Even a company like Google — with its legendary campuses full of perks — has a median tenure of just 1.1 years.

> **Freedom to work from anywhere at any time beats fun vanity perks any day, and it costs our company $0 to provide.**

## But! You Still Need Synchronous Communication Too

At the beginning of Doist's async journey, they got rid of most meetings thinking they were a waste of time. But they learned that **they had lost the human element** — people felt disconnected and missed seeing colleagues face-to-face.

> "We are human after all" — Daft Punk

### How Doist Builds Personal Connections

- **Monthly 1-on-1s** with direct reports to touch base, discuss roadblocks, set professional development goals
- **Monthly casual team video hangouts** where people from different teams chat about non-work things
- **Weekly team meetings** focused on building rapport rather than giving status updates (using red/yellow/green checkins)
- **Yearly company-wide and smaller team retreats**
- **New team member fly-outs** to spend a week working in-person with their team mentor
- **Annual meet-up perk** for getting together with teammates outside of organized retreats
- **Coworking space reimbursement**

### Doist's Communication Stack

- **70% async** using Twist, Github, Google Docs, Figma, and Todoist
- **25% sync** using Zoom, Around, or Google Meet
- **5% in-person** meetups, e.g., annual company and smaller team retreats

### When to Use Synchronous Communication

- You want to build rapport with people (e.g., a 1-on-1 or team meeting)
- You need to provide critical feedback or discuss other sensitive topics
- You have a lot of unknowns and you want to brainstorm different ideas
- There are a lot of moving variables and you want to bring everyone on the same page quickly (e.g., project kickoff)
- A crisis happens that requires immediate attention (use emergency channels like Telegram)

**Synchronous communication should be the exception, not the rule.**

## How to Move Your Team Toward Asynchronous Communication

### Things You Can Do Individually

**1. Overcommunicate**
Include as much information as possible. Use screenshots or screen recordings. Be clear about what you need and what the deadline is.

| Under-communication | Over-communication |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| "Can you please provide me with reporting on content once you get a chance?" | "Can you please provide me with a short report (a few paragraphs) on our blog metrics from Google Analytics for next Tuesday by 2pm EST? I'd like to know top posts, unique page views, bounce rate, and conversation rate. Thanks!" |

**2. Plan ahead to give people time**
"I want to finish this in 2 days and would love your input" instead of "I need your feedback in the next hour."

**3. Say no to unnecessary meetings**
Useful scripts from Dropbox:
> "Thanks for including me! I'm wondering if we could try to solve this in a Twist thread instead?"

> "I've been in so many meetings lately, but I'm trying to be more disciplined about my schedule. Could we try to solve this without a meeting, first?"

> "I'd be happy to give you feedback on that! Before we schedule a meeting, could I review it in a Google Doc/Figma/issue?"

**4. Before meetings, start a thread or document**
Share all relevant information and discuss key issues before the meeting.

**5. After meetings, document discussions and outcomes**
Start or continue a thread so people who weren't there can find that information. Consider recording meetings.

**6. Turn off notifications**
Try removing work apps from your phone altogether. Set aside specific time blocks to check and respond to messages.

**7. Use waiting time productively**
There's always something else to work on.

**8. Always check your document sharing settings**
If someone needs to request access, it can lead to hours or even a full day of delay.

### Things You Can Do as a Team Leader

**1. Promote writing and communication as core skills**
Everyone needs to be a great writer in an async workplace.

**2. Evaluate people based on output and results**
Not how responsive they are or the number of hours they work.

**3. Abolish required work hours or requirements to come into an office**
This will naturally move your organization towards async communication.

**4. Emphasize trust, organization, independence, and accountability**
Without these values, asynchronous communication will never work.

**5. Adopt a Direct Responsible Individual (DRI) model**
A single person is responsible for any given area or project. The more you can decrease the number of people involved in decisions, decentralize authority, and increase individual accountability, the more efficient your team will be.

**6. Set reasonable, team-wide expectations for acceptable response times**
At Doist, they expect people to answer within 24 hours.

**7. Make transparency and documentation a priority**
Everyone at Doist can read all of the core discussions regardless of which team it is. This includes leadership discussions.

**8. Use tools that promote transparency, deep work, and async communication**
Don't use email internally. Email locks information inside people's inboxes where no one else can find it.

**9. For communication-heavy roles, implement a rotating coverage system**
During DND (do-not-disturb) time, a teammate covers for you on incoming client comms so you can truly have undisturbed time.

**10. Have communication channels for emergencies**
Doist uses Telegram with notifications turned on for truly urgent incidents — a few times per year.
