# Communicating Effectively and Responsibly Through Text

*Source: GitLab Handbook - https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/company/culture/all-remote/effective-communication/*

As an all-remote company that defaults to asynchronous communication, GitLab relies heavily on textual communication. This can feel unusual or even uncomfortable for those who come from a colocated environment, where in-person meetings and vocalized communiques are the norm.

Embracing text communication and learning to use it effectively requires a mental shift. Mastering this should be seen as a long-term project, though there are tips to breaking down barriers and experiencing benefits.

---

## Tips for Better Writing

Writing is a skill which can be learned. *Great* writing involves mastering proven principles and understanding how narratives influence thoughts and actions. Below are principles commonly linked to Amazon writing procedures, useful for anyone communicating professionally.

1. **Use short sentences**. Ideally, sentences should have fewer than 15 words. This improves clarity. The Hemingway Editor helps achieve this.

2. **Cut common phrases**. Example: replace "due to the fact" with "because."

3. **Replace adjectives with data**. Hard figures trump descriptors like "most" or "the majority."

4. **Eliminate ambiguous words**. Actively avoid words which weaken your point (Example: "nearly" instead of an exact percentage)

5. **Use the "So What?" test**. Before sending a communique, re-read and ask "So, what?" Does your content provide value? Will your audience understand? Are you sharing enough context?

6. **Avoid adverbs**. If you spot an adverb, remove it. Example: replace "increased massively" with "added 300 new subscribers."

7. **Be objective**. Objectivity injects confidence. Example: replace "I think this change is working" with "our new approach led to a 5% increase."

8. **Cut acronyms and jargon**. Aim to explain to a child. Example: do not assume that someone understands what MR means. Instead, write out "merge request."

9. **Use Subject-Verb-Object sentences**. Follow this path: Who/what are you writing about? → What did they do? → What was acted on?

## Why Text Communication is Important for Successful Remote Working

In an all-remote organization with team members spread across an array of time zones, communicating through text is ideal. Not only is it inclusive and considerate, but a bias towards communicating through text creates a company that documents everything.

**Documentation is an essential competitive advantage.** Companies which rely on vocalizing end up repeating themselves over and over in meetings, creating an inefficient environment with tremendous opportunity for knowledge leaks.

While communicating and documenting through text may feel like a burden in the moment, it prevents a toxic cycle of meetings and touch points which serve only to "bring people up to speed."

> "Great writing makes remote work better. It saves time, reduces meetings, removes extrovert bias, and invites other perspectives."
> — Tim Casasola, Sanctuary Computer

**Communicating answers via text makes documentation easier.** Rather than transcribing voice, one must simply copy and paste in a structured format within a company's handbook. It's vital to understand that nurturing this habit serves more than the person receiving the answer. It is, in essence, **paying it forward**.

Documenting solutions makes a more efficient future for:
- Every new hire who joins and asks the same question
- Existing team members who join a project midstream and need to understand steps taken thus far (and why)

### Additional Reasons to Master Textual Communication

1. Allows team members to work **asynchronously**
2. Information is **available** to all at the same time / immediate
3. Allows for **deliberate** thought and processing time
4. Inherently **inclusive** (works with screen readers and translation tools)
5. Easily **queried** via keyboard or script
6. Can be structured and formatted to showcase **a train of thought**

## Understanding Low-Context Communication

At GitLab, we communicate assuming others have low context. We provide as much context as possible to avoid confusion.

1. **The goal of low context is to be considerate** of the people or audience you're speaking to. Recognize that what you write may be read in the future, or by someone coming into the conversation later.

2. **This is challenging**, particularly for those from colocated spaces. In colocated environments, high context communication is the default — less direct with emphasis on human relations, more sensitive to non-verbals and feelings of others.

3. **Getting in a low context frame of mind**: Start by assuming recipients **do not know anything** about the topic at hand, and wish to learn as much as possible in as little time as possible.

4. **Be precise.** It's easy to imply your experiences with text, but not everyone has similar life experiences to relate to.

5. **Being specific forces you to think through what you're saying.** There is value in **taking the time to be deliberate** about communications. Re-read communiques before sending, particularly if they may be perceived as negative or inflammatory.

6. **Be clear in proposals, especially with trade-offs.** Don't choose between revenue versus usage or broad versus deep without fuller context. To have buy-in and alignment, get into the specifics. Be **clear on the tradeoffs (do A or B) and the proposal (do A but not B)**.

7. **Put yourself in another person's shoes** and try to understand their current perspective and worldview. Text communication is a means to listen, understand, and collaborate — not impose your will.

8. **This doesn't mean cold or soulless.** In the GitLab #thanks Slack channel, we encourage team members to be specific about what they are thanking someone for, such that you don't need prior context to understand how a value was being lived.

## Why Text Communication Can Be Difficult

**The internet is forever.** Words have impact long after they are written, and even when you're communicating internally, the manner in which you speak with one another should be viewed through an external lens.

> Words have meaning, meanings have effects, and effects have consequences. Those consequences, good or bad, are the lasting impact of the words you choose.

For those who struggle with this, consider what you would type if a journalist, your manager, or a family member were in the room observing the conversation.

### Challenges of Text Communication

1. Cultural and communication style differences
2. Delay in immediate feedback (due to time zones or other meetings)
3. Lack of non-verbals
4. Emotional distance
5. Tough to fully consider the scope of your audience(s)

### Understanding Tone in Text-Based Communication

Key points:
1. **Lead conversations with empathy** and assume positive intent
2. **Recognize when text communication isn't working** and talk about it
3. **State your intent clearly and early** in a conversation — leave nothing up to interpretation

## Letting Values Guide Communication

Text communication can be easily derailed, and assumptions can lead to good-mannered communiques being viewed as a slight. GitLab's operating principles are useful when unsure how to communicate effectively:

1. **No ego** — Don't defend a point to win an argument or double-down on a mistake.
2. **Assume positive intent** — If a message feels like a slight, assume positive intent while asking for clarification.
3. **Get to know each other** — Building rapport enables trust.
4. **Say thanks** — Taking every opportunity to share praise creates a climate where feedback is viewed as a gift rather than an attack.
5. **Kindness** — It costs nothing to be kind, even if you do not believe someone deserves it.
6. **It's impossible to know everything** — You can't know how your words are interpreted without asking.
7. **Short toes** — GitLab is a place where others can feel comfortable contributing to others' domains of expertise.

**If someone isn't communicating in the spirit of values**, respectfully ask if they believe they are (in a 1-1 setting). It's easiest to assume the worst and quietly disengage, but doing so creates additional problems:
1. The person communicating is not given the gift of feedback, so their perspective won't change.
2. The concerned person remains timid about communicating, which can lead to toxicity and dysfunction.

## The Power of Contrition

At the speed of business, it's possible for even the most well-mannered colleagues to say something regrettable when their heart was typing faster than their brain could regulate.

**"Say sorry" is an operating principle at GitLab.** It's simple, yet powerful. Saying sorry via Slack or email, or a quick Zoom call, enables trust to be rebuilt.

When using asynchronous collaboration tools such as GitLab Issues and Merge Requests, feel welcome to reply atop your message if you feel you may have written something offensive. This will send another email notification to those tagged, whereas an edit to your original message will not.

## Feedback is a Gift

Managers should strive to **incorporate positive feedback** into their day-to-day work. Particularly in an all-remote setting, where people can work for days, weeks, or months without seeing a colleague in person, friendly words go a long way.

It's easy to brush positive feedback aside as nonessential, but that is unwise in a remote setting. If the only feedback a report receives is negative — while hearing nothing when they are excelling — they are likely to view feedback as an attack.

Moreover, they're likely to:
- Feel unappreciated and misunderstood
- View critical feedback as something delivered to spite them rather than improve them

**Putting effort into positive feedback as well as negative feedback creates a climate where the latter is taken seriously.**

If you need to deliver critical feedback, consider also offering what a person is doing well. This creates trust that you are viewing things in totality and recognize that **people are not their work**.

Feedback is a mechanism to become more understanding and more compassionate. In remote settings with colleagues from around the globe, feedback enables you to squash misconceptions and become a more understanding human.

### Receiving Feedback

- **Assume positive intent** when receiving feedback. If it feels like an attack, ask for clarification.
- The goal is to create a two-way street of communication, so all parties are heard and work is put in to ensure points are understood.
- **Think through your responses to feedback.** You can't choose how you are spoken to, but you *can* control how you respond.
- If your default reaction is to become combative, consider acknowledging feedback and respectfully asking for time to process. Schedule a follow-up conversation once you've had time to process rationally, not just emotionally.

## It's About Your Impact, Not Your Intention

> We judge ourselves by our intentions, but people judge us by our actions.

In an all-remote setting, have a healthy perspective on words you type. Ask for clarification, and ask if your communication could be delivered in a different or more effective way.

For managers of multiple people, you may find that one report prefers a certain delivery while another has a different ideal. **There are multiple ways to deliver a message. Similarly, there are multiple ways to interpret a message.** Be proactive to ensure intentions are delivered.

**When communicating, it's your responsibility to own how something impacted someone, regardless of how you intended it.** If you're surprised by how a communique impacts someone, ask for clarity without being combative. These moments are ideal for learning about yourself and how to tweak your approach to be more considerate.
