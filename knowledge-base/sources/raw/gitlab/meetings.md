How to conduct meetings at GitLab

## How to Run an Effective Meeting at GitLab

We're thoughtful about how we run meetings, because, when done right, they are forums to increase efficiency, enhance collaboration, and drive results.

### What to Do Before the Meeting

1. **Ask yourself if the meeting is necessary.** A strategic balance between synchronous and asynchronous communication is required. Moving a project forward asynchronously when feasible is preferred.

2. **Review participants' time zones.** When a meeting is necessary, review all participants' time zones. Consider creating meetings that overlap between EMEA/APAC + APAC/AMER + AMER/EMEA.

3. **Ensure meeting DRI.** Nominate volunteers to host and facilitate meetings in their working hours.

4. **Send the meeting invite with proper advance notice.** Send invites 72 hours (minimum 24 hours) in advance. Mark non-essential participants as optional.

5. **Include a link to an agenda document.** Meeting agendas should be shared 72 hours (minimum 24 hours) in advance.

6. **Provide slides and supporting videos in advance.** We embrace Neurodiversity by providing multiple ways to consume information (written/video/audio).

7. **Use "speedy meetings."** Google Calendar setting creates meetings of 25/50 minute duration, leaving time to write notes, stretch, etc.

8. **Use the Zoom scheduler plug-in.** Automatically adds Zoom links to events.

9. **Check your audio quality.** Research shows that high audio quality makes people judge content as better. Invest in equipment like microphones.

### What to Do During the Meeting

1. **Start on time.** Begin and end meetings on time to minimize disruption.

2. **Recording.** For group meetings with multiple time zones, consider recording for async participation.

3. **Give audio and video feedback.** Let people know if you can't see or hear them.

4. **Name pronunciation.** Tips to ensure name pronunciation:
   - Ask participants: "My name is pronounced...you can call me..."
   - Introduce yourself with pronouns and pronunciation
   - Add team members' name pronunciation in agenda
   - It's always okay to ask for a reminder on pronunciation
   - If mispronounced: "Thank you for attempting, it's actually pronounced..."
   - Complete Slack and Google profile name pronunciation

5. **Avoid presenting in most meetings.** Synchronous time should not be used for presenting. Recorded presentations make content accessible.

6. **Be intentional about introductions.** If doing introductions, ensure participants are prepared. Create a list of people with roles.

7. **Provide verbal cues.** Add context: "bullet 3c, Sam, you added that point, can you verbalize it?"

8. **Adhere to the agenda order.** Ensure discussions are equitable - don't let anyone monopolize at expense of others.

9. **Document in real-time.** Follow Live Doc meeting best practices.

10. **End on time.**

### What to Do After the Meeting

1. **Capture action items from notes.** Use tags like "TODO" and "DONE" to track action items. File issues in GitLab.com.

2. **Consider important decisions.** When outside some participants' time zones, consider confirming decisions for 24-48 hours for async contribution.

3. **Upload recording.** Add recording link and password within 12 hours.

4. **Assess and Iterate.** Reflect on what worked, what didn't, and what to do differently.

## How Do You Run Inclusive Meetings?

### Avoid Color Coding - Use Inclusive Symbols Instead

Color coding (red/yellow/green status indicators) creates barriers for participants with color vision deficiencies, affecting approximately 8% of men and 0.5% of women globally.

### Why to Avoid Color-Only Indicators

- **Color blindness impacts millions** - Red-green color blindness affects many people
- **Screen settings vary** - Monitor calibration and filters can alter perception
- **Cultural meanings differ** - Colors carry different significance across cultures
- **Cognitive load increases** - Processing color meanings requires extra mental effort
- **Accessibility tools may not translate** - Screen readers cannot convey color

### Inclusive Alternatives to Use Instead

- **Text labels**: "Complete", "In Progress", "Not Started"
- **Shapes with meaning**: ✓ (checkmark), ⟳ (in progress), ○ (not started)
- **Icons and symbols**: ✅ approved, ⚡ urgent, ⏸️ paused, 🔄 in progress
- **Patterns and textures**: Solid fill for complete, diagonal lines for in progress
- **Combination approaches**: Use both color AND symbols
- **Progress indicators**: "25%", "50%", "75%", "100%"
- **Status words**: "Draft", "Review", "Final" or "Open", "Blocked", "Closed"

## 10 Ways to Do All-Remote Meetings Right

### 1. Make Meeting Attendance Optional

When you work in a global all-remote company, the usual assumptions about availability are opposite the norm. We do not look to a meeting by default; when necessary, we strive to make in-person attendance optional by enabling asynchronous contribution.

We are not a democratic or consensus driven company. People are encouraged to give comments and opinions, but one person decides after listening to all feedback.

**Record meetings to encourage async participation.** Record all meetings to allow team members to catch up.

**Managing meeting attendance for team members:**
- Some folks may have **too many meetings**. Help by marking them as optional.
- Some folks may have **few meetings**. They shouldn't attend just for connection - use informal communication alternatives instead.

### 2. Cancel Unnecessary Meetings

Meetings should not happen if there isn't a clear objective and adequate preparation. Roughly 1% of meetings should end within the first minute when it's clear there's no known outcome.

At the end of each meeting, the DRI should evaluate whether the meeting can be **cancelled** or the cadence/duration can be **reduced**.

### 3. Start on Time, End on Time

A nontrivial amount of time is required to regain focus after a distraction. Begin and end meetings on time to minimize disruption.

Use "speedy meetings" setting - 25 or 50 minutes - leaving time to write notes, stretch, etc.

### 4. All Meetings Must Have an Agenda

**"No agenda, no attenda."** Every work-related meeting should have a single live doc agenda affixed to the calendar invite.

**Meeting agendas should include:**
- A relevant meeting title
- Attendees with titles and organization names
- Background Information or context
  - What do folks need to know to meaningfully participate?
  - Why is this group gathering? What's the desired outcome?
- The date (use dates as H1s or H2s for multiple meetings)
- Questions or details where participants can add thoughts
- Link to recording after the meeting

**Meeting DRIs should prep the agenda at least 72 hours before.** This helps participants:
- Make informed decisions about synchronous participation
- Have adequate time for review
- Have time to contribute asynchronously

### 5. Document Everything Live (Yes, Everything)

It's not rude to focus on documentation. A surefire way to waste time is to avoid writing anything down.

- Add input and feedback from attendees to existing agenda items
- Write first and last names or use tagging when referencing team members
- Go directly to a GitLab issue or merge request for action items
- Tag optional attendees or those who couldn't attend in resulting issues
- Record the meeting - you can delete unwanted recordings but can't record retroactively

### 6. Use the Right Tools

- **Zoom** for video calls and screen sharing with simple recording
- **GitLab Issues** to document action items and loop in others
- **Calendly** to show busy status in chat tools

**Use GitLab to replace meetings.** GitLab is a collaboration tool designed to help people work better together.

### 7. Avoid Hybrid Calls

A hybrid call has a mix of participants in the same room with others who are remote. These should be avoided - better to have everyone on a level playing field.

If hybrid must happen, everyone should use their own equipment (camera, headset, screen) even if physically in the same room. Ideally, separate briefly for the call and find your own workspace.

### 8. Meetings Are About the Work, Not the Background

> **Enjoy the benefits of your kids barging in on a meeting. That's the best distraction in the world.** - GitLab co-founder Sid Sijbrandij

- Don't waste time finding the perfect backdrop
- Celebrate unique surroundings - GitLab team members join from lakes, coffee shops, RVs, while walking
- Focus on internet connection and audio quality (use headphones!)
- Encourage family and housemates to say hello!
- Consider *not* using a video call

### 9. It's OK to Look Away

We empower team members to be the manager of their attention.

- It's completely acceptable to work on other tasks if a portion doesn't apply to you
- It's not embarrassing to ask for something to be repeated
- It feels rude to interrupt in video calls due to latency, but we should not be discouraged - questions and context from interruptions are valuable
- Everyone is encouraged to interrupt the speaker to ask questions or offer context

### 10. Say Thanks and Be Creative

GitLab has a dedicated Slack channel devoted to saying thanks. Kindness is embedded in our Collaboration value, and gratitude is essential to our culture.

Example: "How we turned a dull weekly all-hands into a podcast"

Note: We do not thank each other for meeting participation, as this is not part of our values and could reward attending unnecessary meetings.

## Remote Meeting FAQs

### What Are the Benefits of a Remote Meeting?

- Set meeting length and invite limited people
- Participate from almost anywhere in the world
- Use green screens for professional backgrounds while working anywhere

### What Are the Challenges of a Remote Meeting?

- May need to flex schedule for other time zones
- Two main categories:
  1. Trouble with technology
  2. Communication complications

Both can be overcome by checking internet connection regularly and understanding communication preferences of others.

### How Can I Prepare for a Remote Meeting?

1. Review calendar at least a day in advance
2. Carefully read the live doc agenda BEFORE the meeting
3. Check technology, Internet, and environment
4. Don't be afraid to ask for help

### What Should I Do After a Remote Meeting?

1. Write everything down to retain important details
2. Schedule action items, next steps, or future meetings
3. Input into proper directories (Google Drive, Salesforce, etc.)
4. Re-watch video recording if you missed something

### How Do You Create a Low Meeting Culture?

- Make it effective for participants to contribute asynchronously
- Don't thank people for showing up in meetings
- At GitLab, **results** and **efficiency** are values, participation is not

### How Do You Keep Participants Engaged?

- Have video on at all times
- Use team members' names
- Advise participants to mute mics
- Don't be discouraged by interruptions
- Speak up when something isn't working
- Start and end on time
- Give option to ask someone else to "please verbalize" for them

### What Is the Difference Between Remote and In-Person Meetings?

> We can [bring people together physically], but we don't because it's very inefficient. Imagine the cost if I had to fly to your location, or you to mine.
>
> I bet our meetings via Zoom are more efficient than meetings in a conference room. With live note-taking, up-front agendas, and our follow-up, GitLab meetings are more efficient.
>
> There are benefits to colocated meetings - easier to interrupt, see what everyone's thinking, no wireless issues, easier to break bread. We try to take advantage with GitLab Contribute and quarterly executive offsites.
>
> The biggest thing is taking the initiative to interrupt each other, as that's harder remotely. We solve that by having questions in a Google Doc in advance.
> — GitLab co-founder Sid Sijbrandij

### Who Should Be Invited to Meetings?

Use the DRI framework:
- **DRIs and consulted folks play key roles** within meetings
- **Most informed people should not be invited** if there's a more efficient way to cascade information

### How Do You Enable Async Participation?

When agendas are shared at least 72 hours in advance, you can have efficient asynchronous participation. If sticking to the agenda becomes the norm, folks who have thoughts but choose to prioritize other things can have confidence their contributions will be considered.

## All-Remote Virtual "Offsites"

What we've learned:
- Block off everyone's calendar as if they'd traveled
- Ensure a strong desired outcome before planning
- Coordinate pre-reading and async work ahead of time
- Include ice-breakers for newly formed groups
- Schedule breaks during calls longer than 1 hour
- Take more time to explain activities with additional context
- If possible, share a remote meal together coffee-chat style
- Check your tools can support the proposed setup

All-remote virtual offsites are cost-effective, but there is still great value in in-person interactions.
