# GitLab Values

*Source: GitLab Handbook - https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/values/*

## CREDIT

GitLab's six core values are **🤝 Collaboration**, **📈 Results for Customers**, **⏱️ Efficiency**, **🌐 Diversity, Inclusion & Belonging**, **👣 Iteration**, and **👁️ Transparency**, and together they spell the **CREDIT** we give each other by assuming good intent.

---

## 🤝 Collaboration

To achieve results, team members must work together effectively. At GitLab, helping others is a priority, even when it is not immediately related to the goals that you are trying to achieve. Similarly, you can rely on others for help and advice—in fact, you're expected to do so. Anyone can chime in on any subject, including people who don't work at GitLab.

### Operating Principles for Collaboration

#### Kindness
We value caring for others. Demonstrating we care for people provides an effective framework for challenging directly and delivering feedback. Kindness doesn't mean holding back on feedback or avoiding disagreements, these are crucial to professional growth and getting results for customers. Kindness means you make a separation between the work and the person, you can criticize someone's work but still be respectful to the person.

#### Share
Be willing to invest in people and engage in open dialogue. Consider making private issues public wherever possible so that we can all learn from the experience. Don't be afraid of judgement or scrutiny when sharing publicly.

#### Negative feedback is 1-1
Give negative feedback in the smallest setting possible. One-on-one video calls are preferred. Negative feedback is distinct from negativity and disagreement.

#### Provide feedback in a timely manner
We want to solve problems while they are **small**. If you are unhappy with anything, please voice your concerns rather than keeping them to yourself.

#### Say thanks
Recognize the people that helped you publicly, for example in the #thanks chat channel.

#### Give feedback effectively
When providing feedback, always make it about the work itself; focus on the business impact and not the person. Make sure to provide at least one clear and recent example.

#### Get to know each other
We use a lot of text-based communication, and if you know the person behind the text, it will be easier to prevent conflicts. So we encourage people to get to know each other on a personal level through informal communication, for example, virtual coffee chats.

#### Don't pull rank
If you have to remind someone of the position you have in the company, you're doing something wrong. Explain why you're making the decision, and respect everyone irrespective of their function.

#### Assume positive intent
We naturally have a double standard when it comes to the actions of others. We blame circumstances for our own mistakes, but individuals for theirs. This is called the Fundamental Attribution Error. Always assume positive intent in your interactions with others. When disagreeing, try to argue against the strongest version of your opponent's position (steel man, not straw man).

#### Address behavior, but don't label people
We believe that **jerk** is a label for behavior rather than an inherent classification of a person. We avoid classifications.

#### Say sorry
If you made a mistake, apologize as soon as possible. Saying sorry is not a sign of weakness but one of strength.

#### No ego
Don't defend a point to win an argument or double-down on a mistake. You are not your work; you don't have to defend your point. You do have to search for the right answer with help from others.

#### See others succeed
Everyone here mentioned wanting to see each other succeed.

#### Don't let each other fail
Keep an eye out for others who may be struggling or stuck. If you see someone who needs help, reach out and assist.

#### People are not their work
Always make suggestions about examples of work, not the person. Say "You didn't respond to my feedback about the design" instead of "You never listen".

#### Do it yourself
Our collaboration value is about helping each other when we have questions, need critique, or need help. No need to brainstorm, wait for consensus, or do with two what you can do yourself.

#### Blameless problem solving
Investigate mistakes in a way that focuses on the situational aspects of a failure's mechanism and the decision-making process that led to the failure, rather than cast blame on a person or team.

#### Short toes
People joining the company frequently say, "I don't want to step on anyone's toes." At GitLab, we should be more accepting of people taking initiative in trying to improve things. Have short toes and feel comfortable letting others contribute to your domain.

#### It's impossible to know everything
We know we must rely on others for the expertise they have that we don't. It's OK to admit you don't know something and to ask for help. Don't display surprise when people say they don't know something.

#### Collaboration is not consensus
Collaboration is not consensus and disagreement is part of collaboration. You don't need to ask people for their input, and they shouldn't ask you "Why didn't you ask me?". You don't have to wait for people to provide input. They can disagree, commit, and advocate.

## 📈 Results for Customers

We exist to help our customers achieve more. Everything we do should be in service of making our customers successful with GitLab. Results for Customers is at the top of our values hierarchy.

### Operating Principles for Results

#### Set Ambitious & Measurable goals
While we iterate with small changes, we strive for large, ambitious results. Setting ambitious, measurable goals enables us to best deliver customer results.

#### Understand our customers
All GitLab team members should understand our customers' needs, issues, and value propositions.

#### Co-create
We create together with our customers. There is an open dialogue between GitLab and our customers so that we can better identify what they need.

#### Keep end users in sight
Focus on platform enhancements that drive the most value for direct users. Be aware of the Concur effect (decision makers are not direct users, features are overwhelming and disjointed, user experience gets worse over time).

#### Measure impact, not activity
We care about what you achieve: the code you shipped, the needle you moved, the user you made happy. Someone who took the afternoon off shouldn't feel like they did something wrong.

#### Dogfooding
We use our own product in the way our users do to surface improvements that will lead to better customer results.

#### Give agency
We give people agency to focus on what they think is most beneficial.

#### Challenger mindset
A challenger mindset requires that we continually ask ourselves bold, difficult questions about our business and the problems we solve.

#### Growth mindset
We believe our talents can be developed through hard work, targeted training, learning from others, on-the-job experience, and receiving input from others.

#### Cross-functional optimization
Do what is best for the organization as a whole. Don't optimize for the goals of your team when it negatively impacts the goals of other teams.

#### Embrace Tenacity
Tenacity is the ability to display commitment to what you believe in. You keep picking yourself up, dusting yourself off, and quickly get going again.

#### Have Ownership & Accountability
We expect team members to complete tasks that they are assigned. Take initiative and proactively inform stakeholders.

#### Sense of urgency
Time gained or lost has compounding effects. Try to get the results as fast as possible.

#### Operate with a bias for action
It's important that we keep our focus on action, and don't fall into the trap of analysis paralysis.

#### Disagree, commit, and advocate
When a decision is in place, we expect people to commit to executing it. Any past decisions and guidelines are open to questioning as long as you act in accordance with them until they are changed.

#### Escalate to unblock
If there is a disagreement and you can't move forward, agree to escalate. Early escalation, delivered with context of the challenge, enables managers to function as an unblocker.

## ⏱️ Efficiency

Efficiency means producing results without wasting materials, time, or energy. We optimize solutions globally for the broader GitLab community over one person or a small group.

### Operating Principles for Efficiency

#### Only Healthy Constraints
Most companies regress to the mean and slow down over time. We try to limit ourselves to healthy constraints.

#### Write things down
We document everything: in the handbook, in meeting notes, in issues. "The faintest pencil is better than the sharpest memory." It is far more efficient to read a document at your convenience than to have to ask and explain.

#### Boring solutions
Use the simplest and most boring solution for a problem. The speed of innovation for our organization and product is constrained by the total complexity we have added so far.

#### Self-service and self-learning
Team members should first search for their own answers and, if an answer is not readily found, ask in public. Write down any new information discovered and pay it forward.

#### Efficiency for the right group
Optimize solutions globally for the broader GitLab community. Ask yourself "For whom does this need to be most efficient?"

#### Be respectful of others' time
Consider the time investment you are asking others to make with meetings and a permission process. Try to avoid meetings.

#### Spend company money like it's your own
Every dollar we spend will have to be earned back. Be as frugal with company money as you are with your own.

#### Frugality
"Accomplish more with less. Constraints breed resourcefulness, self-sufficiency, and invention."

#### Short verbal answers
Give short answers to verbal questions so the other party has the opportunity to ask more or move on.

#### Keep broadcasts short
Keep one-to-many written communication short.

#### Managers of one
We want each team member to be a manager of one who doesn't need daily check-ins to achieve their goals.

#### Freedom and responsibility over rigidity
When possible, we give people the responsibility to make a decision and hold them accountable for that, instead of imposing rules and approval processes.

#### Accept mistakes
Not every problem should lead to a new process to prevent them. Additional processes make all actions more inefficient.

#### Move fast by shipping the minimal valuable change
We value constant improvement by iterating quickly. If a task is not the smallest viable and valuable thing, cut the scope.

#### Embrace change
Adoption of features, user requirements, and the competitive landscape change frequently. We should adapt our roadmap and our organization quickly.

## 🌐 Diversity, Inclusion & Belonging

Diversity, inclusion and belonging are fundamental to the success of GitLab. We aim to make a significant impact in our efforts to foster an environment where everyone can thrive.

### Operating Principles for DIB

#### Bias towards asynchronous communication
Take initiative to operate asynchronously whenever possible. This shows care and consideration for those who may not be in the same time zone.

#### Embracing uncomfortable ideas and conversations
Part of embracing diversity is a willingness to embrace often uncomfortable conversations and situations. Challenge yourself and your own pre-set notions.

#### Understanding the impact of microaggressions
Microaggressions can wear people down by slowly chipping away their sense of belonging/safety/inclusion over time.

#### Seek diverse perspectives
Team members seeking feedback from a diverse group leads to better decisions and a greater sense of belonging.

#### Make family feel welcome
If the tenor of the meeting allows, feel welcome to invite your family members or pets to drop by and greet your colleagues.

#### Shift working hours for a cause
If there's a cause or community effort taking place, feel welcome to work with your manager and shift your working hours.

#### Be a mentor
People feel more included when they're supported.

#### Culture fit is a bad excuse
We don't hire based on culture or select candidates because we'd like to have a drink with them. We want a **values fit**, not a culture fit. We want cultural diversity instead of cultural conformity.

#### Religion and politics at work
We generally avoid discussing politics or religion in public forums because it is easy to alienate people that have a minority opinion.

#### Quirkiness
Unexpected and unconventional things make life more interesting. Celebrate and encourage quirky gifts, habits, behavior, and points of view.

#### Building a safe community
Do **not** make jokes or unfriendly remarks about characteristics of the people who make up GitLab. Everyone has the right to feel safe.

#### Unconscious bias
We recognize that unconscious bias is something that affects everyone and we are responsible for understanding our own implicit biases.

#### Inclusive language & pronouns
Use **inclusive** language. For example, prefer "Hi everybody" or "Hi people" to "Hi guys", and "they" instead of "he/she".

#### Learn how to pronounce other people's names
We attach part of our identity to our names, and if it is mispronounced it can feel less inclusive.

#### Inclusive interviewing
Documented on the page about interviewing.

#### Inclusive meetings
Be consciously inclusive in meetings by giving everyone present an opportunity to talk and present their points of view.

#### See Something, Say Something
It is important for each of us to use great judgment in being respectful and inclusive of our teammates. Speak up when you see something that isn't respectful or inclusive.

#### Embracing Neurodiversity
We embrace Neurodiversity through adopting a variety of different work styles and communication styles.

#### Family and friends first, work second
Long-lasting relationships are the rocks of life, and come before work. **#FamilyAndFriends1st**

#### Equity not just equality
Equity recognizes that each person has different circumstances and allocates the exact resources and opportunities needed to reach an equal outcome.

## 👣 Iteration

At GitLab, we iterate to do the smallest valuable thing to get fast feedback and efficiently reach a desired end goal.

### Operating Principles for Iteration

#### Start with a long-term vision
Iteration involves driving results in pursuit of a long-term vision. Shipping that vision in iterations allows us to learn from customers and adjust.

#### Iteration is no substitute for planning
A plan should include: Time-bound objective, UX, Quality, Success metric, Data schema, GTM plan, Enablement, Marketing, Secure by design.

#### The release process is not iteration
Moving through the release process (dogfooding, early access, feature flag, development stage, release, announcement) is not itself iteration.

#### Iterate toward global maximum
If we are not aware of interdependencies beyond our team, we risk deliverables that settle into a "local maximum."

#### Don't wait
When you have something of value like a potential blog post or a small fix, implement it straight away. Inspiration is perishable.

#### Set a due date
We always try to set a due date. If needed, we cut scope.

#### Cleanup over sign-off
Waiting for approval can slow things down. We can prevent this with automation or clean-up after the fact.

#### Start off by impacting the fewest users possible
If you do a gradual rollout of your change, prefer few users over many, internal users over external, fast feedback environments over slow.

#### Reduce cycle time
Short iterations reduce our cycle time. Merging frequently also prevents merge conflicts.

#### Work as part of the community
Small iterations make it easier to work with the wider community.

#### Minimal Valuable Change (MVC)
We encourage MVCs to be as small as possible. Always look to make the quickest change possible to improve the user's outcome.

#### Make a proposal
If you need to decide something as a team, make a concrete proposal instead of calling a meeting to get everyone's input. Every meeting should be a review of a proposal.

#### Everything is in draft
At GitLab, we rarely mark any content or proposals as drafts. Everything is always in draft and subject to change.

#### Under construction
Educating users on the longer-term plan helps create a shared understanding of how a small change will incrementally grow into something more.

#### Low level of shame when dogfooding
In many organizations, you take a risk when you put forth any work that's not perfect. Having a low level of shame requires you to celebrate the small changes.

#### Cultural lens
Cultural differences can bring unique challenges and expectations to iteration. Encourage you to bring your authentic self and seek shared understanding.

#### Focus on improvement
We believe great companies sound negative because they focus on what they can improve. We are **S**hort **Te**rm **C**ritical **A**nd **L**ong **T**erm **O**ptimistic (`STeCALTO`).

#### Be deliberate about scale
First, optimize for speed and results; when it is a success, figure out how to scale it.

#### Resist bundling
Resist the urge to bundle a series of smaller iterations. This reduces the risk that work will be canceled due to scale or scope.

#### Make two-way door decisions
Most decisions are easy to reverse. The DRI should go ahead and make them without approval. Only when you can't reverse them should there be a more thorough discussion.

#### Changing proposals isn't iteration
Changing something without shipping it is a revision, not iteration. Only when the change is rolled out to users can you learn from feedback.

#### Embracing Iteration
We should have the attitude that we are trying to achieve as much as possible in a small amount of time.

#### Make small merge requests
When submitting a merge request, keep it as small as possible.

#### Always iterate deliberately
Rapid iteration can get in the way of results if it's not thought out. In some instances, we add additional review to the approval process.

#### 12 things that are NOT iteration:
1. Reducing quality, or lowering goal posts
2. Avoiding or reducing documentation
3. Compromising on security
4. Delivering something that's not the recommended path
5. Shipping something of no value
6. An excuse to focus on unimportant items
7. Moving through the release process
8. Revisions you don't ship or publish
9. An excuse to impose unrealistically tight timelines
10. An excuse to avoid planning
11. Imposing long hours
12. Expecting others to fix your work

## 👁️ Transparency

Be open about as many things as possible. By making information public, we can reduce the threshold to contribution and make collaboration easier.

### Operating Principles for Transparency

#### Public by default
Everything at GitLab is public by default. We make our values public for many reasons.

#### Not public
We make information public by default because transparency is one of our values. However, a category of information is **public** unless there is a reason for it not to be.

#### Directness
Being direct is about being transparent with each other. We try to be both straightforward and kind.

#### Articulate when you change your mind
If you state one thing, and then change course and support a different direction, articulate this.

#### Surface issues constructively
Be transparent to the right people (up) at the right time (when still actionable).

#### Transparency is most valuable if you continue to do it when there are costs
We practice transparency even when hiding the facts would be easier.

#### Single Source of Truth
By having most company communications and work artifacts be public to the Internet, we have one single source of truth for all GitLab team members.

#### Findability
Transparency means more than just making information accessible. It's important that information flows to the correct places and is findable by those who need it.

#### Say why, not just what
Transparent changes have the reasons for the change laid out clearly along with the change itself.

#### Reproducibility
Enable everybody involved to come to the same conclusion as you. Provide raw data, scripts, and document steps.

## Values Hierarchy

Occasionally, values can contradict each other. The hierarchy helps resolve confusion:

1. **Results** (highest priority)
2. **Collaboration**
3. **Efficiency**
4. **Diversity, Inclusion & Belonging**
5. **Iteration**
6. **Transparency** (lowest in hierarchy, but still very important)

Values higher in the hierarchy do not automatically override values lower. Consider the magnitude of impact when weighing tradeoffs.

## Five Dysfunctions Prevention

Our values prevent the five dysfunctions:

1. **Fear of conflict** → prevented by transparency (directness) and collaboration (short toes)
2. **Absence of trust** → prevented by collaboration (kindness)
3. **Avoidance of accountability** → prevented by results, iteration, and transparency
4. **Inattention to results** → prevented by results
5. **Lack of commitment** → prevented by transparency (directness)

## Permission to Play

From our values we excluded some obvious behaviors:
1. Be truthful and honest.
2. Be dependable and reliable.
3. Try to keep promises. If you might not keep a promise, proactively communicate.
4. Be deserving of trust.
5. Be committed to the success of the whole organization.
6. Act in the best interest of the company.
7. Make the best decisions for GitLab.
8. Act in accordance with the law.
9. Don't show favoritism.

## Playing Politics is Counter to GitLab Values

| Political environment | Non-political environment |
| ------ | ------ |
| Values are weaponized | Team members utilize values with positive intent |
| Team members driven by self-interest | Team members driven by company interest |
| Team members work in silos | Team members optimize globally |
| Territorial behaviors | Short toes |
| Unhealthy alliances with backroom conversations | Good intent and active collaboration |
| Information intentionally withheld | Information shared early (often WIP) |
| People try to undermine each other's credibility | People take a "steel man" position |
| No direct feedback | Feedback is given directly |
| Lack of transparency in escalations | Effective escalations |
