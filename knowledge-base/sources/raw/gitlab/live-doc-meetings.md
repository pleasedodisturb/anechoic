# GitLab Meeting Best Practices: Live Doc Meetings

*Source: GitLab Handbook - https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/company/culture/all-remote/live-doc-meetings/*

On this page, we're detailing how to create efficient, transparent, documentation-based meetings leveraging GitLab's proven principles. This style of meeting increases cohesion, discipline, and transparency regardless of the work environment.

---

## GitLab Meeting Best Practices

**"No agenda, no attenda."**

Every work-related meeting should have a live doc agenda affixed to the calendar invite.

Coffee chats are excluded from this given their function in informal communication.

If you determine that a meeting is needed to move a project forward, address a blocker, or resolve a miscommunication, follow the proven principles below.

---

## Agenda and Sharing

1. **Create the agenda ahead of time** in a Google Doc (or any shared document service)
   - Do NOT use Google Calendar's automatic agenda/notes creation function, as it does not adhere to GitLab's preferred agenda flow

2. **Ensure appropriate sharing settings** to prevent a chorus of "I can't access the agenda" comments
   - Agendas should be **editable by all participants**

3. **Link the agenda in the meeting invite**

4. **Establish the agenda as far in advance of the meeting as possible**

5. **Use numbered lists**, not bulleted lists

6. **Context section includes:**
   - The goal(s) of the meeting
   - Clear pre-work or pre-watch/read instructions
   - Intent and expected outcomes using low-context communication

---

## Pre-Work and Meeting Kick-Off

1. **Pre-read/watch or pre-work materials** should be emailed to attendees at least 24 hours in advance

2. **Meeting host (DRI)** may summarize key points and context for the first 1 to 2 minutes of the meeting, but few meetings should be presentations

3. **Questions should be written and listed in advance** of the scheduled meeting time
   - This tends to increase the quality of questions

---

## Note Taking and Structure During the Meeting

### Make a Proposal
**Make a proposal whenever feasible.** It should be possible to answer/comment with a simple "yes."

### Link Everything
Link any relevant piece of text to associated resources to prevent people from spending time searching or asking.

### Smart Note Taking
Take notes **inline with the agenda** rather than using a separate "notes" section. Real-time note-taking by multiple participants is encouraged.

### Alternating Note Takers
It's helpful if one person takes notes as the first speaker is talking, then another starts when the next speaker continues the conversation. By alternating this way, participants are better able to keep up with all speakers.

### When Not Talking, Help Take Notes
If there isn't a note taker listed in the document at the start of the meeting, people should self-note-take. Consider asking other people to write down the answers in real-time to allow the person who asked the question to focus on the answer.

### Note Structure Guidelines
- Notes should be properly indented and structured to follow the conversation's flow
- Notes do not have to be verbatim, but they should capture key points made and be clear on any decisions or next steps
- **Preface questions and answers with the participant's name**, giving context to the origin

---

## Inclusion and Transparency in Meetings

### Follow the Order
**Follow the order of the questions** instead of jumping around in the agenda. This prevents the proverbial loudest voice in the room from dominating a conversation.

### Verbalize for Others
Always ask if the name preceding the question would like to verbalize. If they wish not to, or are otherwise unavailable, the meeting host should verbalize on their behalf.

### Reenact Questions
**Reenact questions in the list by verbalizing them** even if they were already asked and answered in the shared document.

Why?
- Hearing the question and answer allows everyone to think about it and respond to it
- When verbalizing, more context is often given since most people can talk faster than they can type
- If you're recording the meeting, the recording contains everything

### Record the Meeting
Unless the meeting contains Not Public information:
- **Record the meeting** to share with those who cannot attend live
- Consider livestreaming for maximum transparency and feedback potential

### It's OK to Look Away
If a portion of a meeting does not pertain to you, it's OK to look away. **You are the manager of your own time and attention.**

## Benefits of Live Doc Meetings

Establishing a culture where team members are cognizant that they work with others who may be in a different location, or unable to attend a meeting live, is critical.

**Documentation is a vital part of avoiding team dysfunction.**

### Starting Point for Documentation Culture
If you're looking for a place to start in capturing team-wide buy-in on documentation, consider:
- Adding agendas to all work-related meetings
- Insisting on live documentation during them

### Key Benefits

#### 1. Prevents Knowledge Leaks
Meetings without agendas are only useful to those in them, and even then, it's likely that outcomes will be forgotten, or critical pieces of the puzzle will become less familiar over time.

#### 2. Creates a More Inclusive Meeting Atmosphere
- People can add questions and insights before (and even after) a synchronous meeting
- Those who are less comfortable verbalizing points in front of management can use the agenda doc to properly articulate their complete thoughts

#### 3. Creates a Takeaway
The agenda doc lives longer after the meeting. If you want to share it further — perhaps sending to a person or team that you realize would have benefited from attending — you're able to do so.

#### 4. Shifts the Memorization Burden Away from Humans
If there's an agenda doc affixed to each calendar invite, you can easily search your calendar for key words (marketing, CEO, engineering, etc.), find a given meeting, and immediately access a documented history of what was discussed.

## When Should I Use a Meeting Over Async?

**Not all meetings are inherently bad.** We encourage managers to establish regular 1:1 meetings with their team, for example.

There are instances where a brief synchronous chat can replace multiple hours of asynchronous work.

**Strategically leveraging sync and async is an art, not a science.**

Many meetings can be avoided by understanding how to work well asynchronously.

---

## What is a Live Doc Meeting?

A live doc meeting is a GitLab specialty. It's a work-related meeting (not a coffee chat) where you **document everything live (yes, everything)!**

Start documenting before the meeting begins by attaching a shared, editable agenda document to the calendar invite.

---

## What is the Agenda for a Live Doc Meeting?

Include the agenda for the meeting, set the appropriate sharing permissions, and add instructions for expected engagement (in Context section) so team members understand how to contribute whether they attend synchronously or asynchronously.

### Context Section Should Include:
- Goals of the meeting
- Pre-work instructions
- Expected outcomes
- How to participate (sync or async)

---

## What is Meant by 'Live Document'?

A live document is a document that may continually be edited and updated.

Examples:
- A shared Google Doc
- Whiteboard being used by a team for collaboration
- GitLab's handbook

---

## How Can I Participate in a Live Doc Meeting?

There are two ways to participate:

### 1. Synchronously
Join a live video call (Zoom, etc.)

### 2. Asynchronously
Review the documented discussion along with any recorded video after the meeting is over

---

## When Are Live Doc Meetings Held?

Live doc meetings are usually held in the host's time zone.

However, leaders should:
- **Rotate meetings** to accommodate a more diverse array of time zones
- **Record them** so that others can watch at a later time

---

## Where Are Live Doc Meetings Held?

Live doc meetings can happen:
- Fully in-person
- Fully online
- On a hybrid call (blend of both)

### Important Guidance on Hybrid Calls

**It's best to have everyone on a level playing field** for communication and discussion.

**Hybrid calls should be avoided** whenever possible.

If a hybrid call is necessary:
- Have participants use their own equipment (camera, headset, screen)
- This avoids audio problems from delays and feedback
