# GitLab Communication Handbook

*Source: GitLab Handbook - https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/communication/*

We're an all-remote company that allows people to work from almost anywhere in the world. We hire great people regardless of where they live, but with GitLab team members across more than 60 countries, it's important for us to practice clear communication in ways that help us stay connected and work more efficiently.

To accomplish this, we use **asynchronous communication as a starting point** and stay as open and transparent as we can by communicating through public issues, merge requests, and Slack channels.

We also place an emphasis on ensuring that conclusions of offline conversations are written down.

**When we go back and forth three times, we jump on a synchronous video call.**

---

## Effective & Responsible Communication Guidelines

1. **Assume Positive Intent.** Always begin with a position of positivity and grace.
2. **Kindness Matters.** You are looking at a screen, but you are really talking to a person.
3. **Express Your Thoughts Responsibly and Inclusively.** We live in different locations and often have very different perspectives.
4. **Own It.** If you say it or type it, own it. If it hurts the company or an individual, apologize easily.
5. **Be a Role Model of our Values.**
6. **Feedback is Essential.** We encourage team members to give and receive feedback in a considerate way.
7. **Do not underestimate a 1:1.** Sometimes it's more effective to jump on a Zoom video call.
8. **Always Adhere to our Anti-Harassment Policy and Code of Business Conduct and Ethics.**
9. **Focus on what we can directly influence.**
10. **Commit to active and effective listening.**

### Everyone is a moderator

If you see something that concerns you in Slack, Issues, Merge Requests, Video, Emails or any other forum, we encourage you to respectfully say something directly to the individual in a 1:1 format.

## Asynchronous Communication

In an all-remote setting, mastering asynchronous workflows is vital to avoiding dysfunction and enjoying outsized efficiencies and lifestyle flexibility.

Asynchronous communication is the art of communicating and moving projects forward *without* the need for additional stakeholders to be available at the same time your communique is sent.

### Communicate directly

When working on a problem or issue, communicate directly with the people you need support from rather than working through reporting lines. Direct communication is more efficient than working through your manager, their manager, or another intermediary.

## Communicating Potentially Sensitive Topics

Words have impact long after they are written, and even when you're communicating internally, the manner in which you speak with one another should be viewed through an external lens.

### Confidentiality Levels

At GitLab, we are public by default, but some information is classified as internal or limited access.

### Risks to Consider

1. **Legal risk:** Content that would compromise personal data and/or privacy.
2. **Morale risk:** Raising sensitive topics that may be misinterpreted.
3. **PR risk:** Remember that anything you document could ultimately be shared/viewed externally.

## Internal Communication

### Top Tips and Best Practices

1. All written communication happens in English, even when sent one on one.
2. Use **asynchronous communication** when possible: merge requests (preferred) or issues.
3. Discussion in issues or Merge Requests is preferred over everything else.
4. You are not expected to be available all the time. No expectation to respond outside of planned working hours.
5. Sometimes synchronous communication is better - don't default to it though.
6. It is OK to ask as many questions as you have. Use public channels instead of direct messages.
7. If someone sends a handbook link as an answer, consider adding context for new team members.
8. If the answer isn't documented, immediately make a merge request to add it.
9. Include links when mentioning something (MR, issue, commit, webpage, comment).
10. All company data should be **shareable** by default.
11. When asked something, give back a deadline or confirm you did it.
12. Use CC to bring attention, but explicitly communicate your needs.
13. Avoid creating private groups for internal discussions.
14. Use low-context communications by being explicit. Provide as much context as possible.
15. Don't lean too heavily into hypotheticals.

### Multimodal Communication

Employ multimodal communication to broadcast important decisions:
- Announce in company Slack channel
- Email the appropriate team lists
- Slack the appropriate channels
- Target 1:1s or other important meetings

Create and link to a **single source of truth**: ideally the handbook, otherwise an epic, issue, or Google Doc.

### Start with a Merge Request

When possible, start a discussion with a Merge Request instead of an issue. An MR is associated with a specific change that is proposed and transparent for everyone to review.

1. Always **open** an MR for things you are suggesting and/or proposing.
2. Never ask someone to create an issue when they can default to the merge request.
3. Starting with a Merge Request is part of Handbook First.
4. Merge Requests are **non-confidential** by default.
5. Keep discussions focused by **defining the problem first** and **explaining your rationale**.
6. Have a **bias for action** and do not aim for consensus.
7. Submit the **smallest** viable and valuable thing.
8. Do not leave MRs open for a long time.
9. Respond to comments within a **threaded discussion**.
10. If you received feedback, acknowledge comments.

### Issues

Issues are valuable when there isn't a specific code change proposed:
- Crafting a research proposal
- Ideating on designs
- Breaking down implementation tasks
- Tracking progress

## Meetings

### Common Meeting Problems

Meetings are incredibly expensive since they require synchronous time.

| Problem | Solution |
| ------- | -------- |
| Present instead of Q&A | Pre-record presentations on YouTube, meetings are only Q&A |
| Meetings set up for brainstorming | Default to making thoughtful proposals async |
| No agenda with edit rights | Ensure every meeting has an agenda available for everyone to edit |
| People late or no restroom time | Use Speedy Meetings for breathing space |

### Everyone is Responsible for Notes

If folks are involved in a meeting and have capacity, they should take notes. This is important because:
1. Meetings at GitLab should have notes (for SSOT and async participation)
2. This work disproportionately falls to underrepresented groups without joint commitment

### Smart Note Taking in Meetings

1. If note-taking coverage isn't happening, identify dedicated note-takers
2. Utilize Zoom's AI Companion for meeting summaries
3. Team members should contribute to notes as needed
4. Ask others to scribe answers in real-time
5. Focus on noting speakers and key points
6. Write down questions before vocalizing
7. Use discretion if sensitive topics are discussed
8. Capture key takeaways, next steps, and DRIs at the end

### Few Meetings with Presentations

Presenting during meetings requires valuable synchronous time. Recorded presentations make content accessible.

**Pre-recorded presentations enable:**
- Time for Q&A
- Asynchronous consumption
- Self-service and self-learning
- Greater participation from neurodiverse team members
- Selective watching of material

### Scheduling Meetings

1. Every scheduled meeting should have a Google Presentation or Google Doc linked.
2. For AMER team members collaborating with EMEA: reserve PST morning block for cross-regional collaboration.
3. Send calendar invites with Google Calendar to GitLab accounts.
4. Click 'Guests can modify event' so people can update the time.
5. Use "speedy meetings" setting (25 or 50 minutes) for bio breaks, note writing, etc.
6. Start meetings at :00 or :30 for common start times.
7. Cancel meetings by deleting/declining with "Delete & update guests".

### Video Calls

1. Use video calls if you've gone **back and forth 3 times** - time for a video call.
2. Sometimes it's better to *not* have a video call. Consider:
   - Difficult to multi-task in video calls
   - Async conversation may be more efficient
   - Video calls are limited in time and people
3. Try to have video on - it's more engaging.
4. Not every part of a meeting is relevant.
5. We prefer Zoom.
6. Consider using a utility to easily mute/unmute (like Shush).
7. Hybrid calls are annoying.
8. We start on time and do not wait for people.
9. Everyone is encouraged to interrupt to ask questions or offer context.
10. We end on the scheduled time.
11. Do not use chat in Zoom - use the linked document instead.

#### You Are the Manager of Your Attention

You decide when you do or don't pay attention in a meeting. If you get invited to a meeting you don't think you should attend, decline it. It is better to cancel than to show up and not pay attention.

#### Do Not Ask Attendees to Pay Attention

Don't tell people to give you their attention or stop multi-tasking. Respect each team member's agency over their time. Earn attention by organizing compelling meetings.

## Slack

Slack is used for:
- Internal-only communication and announcements
- Company newsletters and team updates
- Asynchronous team standups, questions, and quick collaboration
- Informal communication

Use a bias for action to move conversations requiring collaboration out of Slack and into issues.

Only 90 days of Slack activity will be retained.

### Slack Should NOT Be Used For:
- Obtaining approvals
- Documenting decisions
- Storing official company records or documents
- Sharing personal or sensitive information

### General Guidelines

1. Everyone can contribute - we value proposals and iteration.
2. Use `:white_check_mark:` emoji to indicate an inquiry has been answered.
3. Use threads to keep conversations together.
4. Don't break up a topic into multiple messages (causes multiple notifications).
5. Agree in calls - person who didn't leave last comment starts the call.

### Avoid Direct Messages

When using Slack for work-related purposes, avoid direct messages. Direct messages discourage collaboration:
- You might be contacting the wrong person
- If the person is unavailable, others cannot help
- Use a public channel and mention the person

If someone sends you a work-related DM, move the conversation to a public channel.

### Use Public Channels

- If you message 3+ people, we recommend a channel for the project/issue.
- If something is important but not urgent, use email or post without @-mentioning.
- It's not rude to leave a channel when you've had your questions answered.

### Be Respectful of Others' Time

- If only referring to someone without needing attention, spell out their name without @.
- Slack messages should be considered asynchronous communication.
- Do not feel obligated to respond when not working.
- **Avoid using @here or @channel unless urgent and important.**

## Writing Style Guidelines

1. Do not use rich text; use Markdown.
2. Do not use ALL CAPS (feels like shouting).
3. Use Unix style (lf) line endings.
4. Include both Metric and Imperial measurements.
5. Monetary amounts shouldn't have one digit ($19.90 not $19.9).
6. US-based team members: use "non-US" instead of "international".
7. Number multiple points in comments or emails.
8. Include URLs when referencing issues, MRs, commits, etc.
9. Prefer hyphens to underscores in URLs, always lowercase.
10. All people working for GitLab are "GitLab team members", not "employees".
11. Use inclusive and gender-neutral language.
12. Always write "GitLab" with capital G and L.
13. If an email needs a response, write the answer at the top.
14. Use the future version of words (frontend, webhook without hyphen).
15. Our homepage is https://about.gitlab.com/ (with "about." and "https").
16. Properly format headers; start at second header level.
17. Do not use acronyms when you can avoid them.

### Communicating Dates and Time

1. For internal content, use ISO dates: `yyyy-mm-dd`
2. For months, use `yyyy-mm`
3. Refer to a year with CY18 and a quarter with CY18-Q1
4. GitLab operates on a Fiscal Year offset from calendar year
5. Don't use "EOD" or "end of day" - specify exact date and time
6. Use timezone references

### Simple Language

Use the most clear, straightforward, and meaningful words possible. Avoid "fluff" words, jargon, or "corporate-speak".

When you **don't** use Simple Language, you:
- Confuse people
- Cause others not to speak up
- Are not inclusive of non-native English speakers
- Do not add value

| Instead of... | Try... |
| ------------- | ------ |
| Getting buy-in/alignment | Asking for feedback since DRIs make decisions |
| Synergy | Effective Collaboration |
| Leverage | Use more explicit phrasing |
| Punt | Make less of a priority |
| When someone has spare cycles | When someone is available |

## Google Docs

Never use a Google Doc for something non-confidential that has to end up on the website or handbook. Work on these edits via commits to a merge request.

### Link Sharing

The recommended defaults when sharing for internal purposes:
- **Visibility**: GitLab (recommended default)
- **Access**: Editor
- **Searchable**: Can find in search results

### Good Practices

1. If content in a Google Doc moves to the website/handbook, deprecate the Google Doc.
2. When referring to a Google Doc in the handbook, indicate the name of the doc rather than directly linking.
3. Search within GitLab domain if having trouble finding shared docs.
4. Documents frequently adapted should be linked; rarely customized content should be on the site directly.
5. Click someone's icon in a Google Doc to jump to their cursor location.
6. Set notifications to "Only yours" to avoid email for every comment.
7. Use Pageless format for documents that won't be printed.

### How to Deprecate a Google Doc

1. Add 'Deprecated: ' to the start of the title.
2. Remove the content you moved.
3. Add a link to the new location at the beginning.
4. Add a link to the merge request or commit that moved it.

## Effective Communication Competency

In an all-remote organization, effective communication is key to exchanging knowledge, ideas, and information.

Effective communication at GitLab:
- Uses asynchronous communication as the starting point
- Places emphasis on ensuring conclusions are written down
- Produces video when necessary

**Skills for Team Members:**
- Effectively practices communication via text
- Uses asynchronous communication when possible
- Directs all communication to appropriate channels
- Recognizes when synchronous communication is more appropriate
- Directs all decisions to the Handbook as SSOT
- Records videos when that's the most efficient way
- Employs multimodal communication for important decisions
- Practices low context communication

**Skills for People Managers:**
- Implements working asynchronously across teams
- Holds team members accountable for effective text communication
- Fosters an environment where async communication is the starting point
- Guides team members on when producing video is appropriate
