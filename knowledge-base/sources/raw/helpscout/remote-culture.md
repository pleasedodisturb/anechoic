## Key Insight: Remote Requires Investment

Successfully building a remote culture requires unwavering commitment from company leaders, and when you get it right, **the benefits compound over time**.

Remote teams require:
- More investment in the people team
- Better communication
- A higher level of proactive transparency than colocated teams

**However, these are all things that benefit the company and its velocity as you grow and scale. Not to mention, you have access to a lot more talent.**

---

## 1. "Remote-First" or Don't Bother

If you want remote company culture to be all or part of your culture mix, **you need to be all in**.

### The RTO Problem

Many companies flip-flop. They praised remote work during the pandemic, only to backpedal with hybrid schedules or rigid return-to-office (RTO) mandates that come with vague promises of "better connection."

> While it's true that closer proximity provides more opportunity for relationship building, **it takes more than getting everyone in the same room once or twice a week to create a good company culture**.

### How Help Scout Did It

When Help Scout had physical offices in Boston and Boulder:
- Neither had a beer fridge, ping pong table, whiteboard, or office perks
- **This was intentional** — whether in-office or at home, everyone had the same benefits
- Focused on good habits in written communication and documentation so everyone had the same information

> **We don't view remote work as an employee perk but rather as a part of our DNA.**

## 2. Slow Down the Hiring Process

Anyone looking for work will tell you that the job market is pretty wild. If we wanted to, we could easily fill our open roles within a matter of days. **But we've learned that speed usually comes at the expense of quality.**

### Help Scout's Process

- **Average ~60 days** to fill a role
- **~1 month** for a candidate to go through the full process
- Includes a **paid project** designed for the person to spend a few hours showing what they can do
- They go back and forth, giving critical feedback and **simulating what it would be like to work together**

### Benefits

- Gives a chance to test the waters
- Wonderful candidate experience — lets them get to know several people on the team
- When the process is more thorough and involves more people, **it's easier to steer clear of unconscious bias**

## 3. Recruit to Diversify the Hiring Pool

The data is abundantly clear: **Diverse companies are more successful.**

According to McKinsey: *"Companies in the top quartile for both gender and ethnic diversity in executive teams are on average nine percent more likely to financially outperform their peers."*

### Why It Matters

Homogenized teams come with **blind spots** that get in the way of creativity and innovation:
- Ad or website copy that misses the mark
- Design choices that make your product less accessible
- Decisions made in an echo chamber

> As a remote team, your opportunity to hire people from underrepresented groups is far greater. **Prioritize hiring a recruiter to source diverse candidates.**

## 4. Seek People Who Love the Work

A common issue at companies where execs demand RTO is a **lack of trust** — managers don't believe their employees are working unless they can see them.

### Help Scout's Approach

Look for people who:
- Love what they do
- Just need a culture that gives them **enough ownership to do their best work**

> When you give people that space — that trust — to follow their intuition, dig into challenges that interest them, and manage their own time, **the results speak for themselves**.

**Focus less on whether someone's status is green on Teams or Slack and more on their impact.**

## 5. Focus on Communication Skills

**A culture's effectiveness revolves around how information flows.**

### The Problem with In-Office Defaults

In-office teams disseminate information via:
- Impromptu meetings
- Whiteboard sessions
- Chats at the water cooler
- Lunch or drinks after work

When you adopt a similar approach remotely — quick huddles without documented outcomes, conversations siloed in DMs — **people will start to feel out of the loop**.

### The Solution

- **Standardize communication** when you go remote
- All company information needs to be **recorded and available for people to consume async**
- Adopt a level of **transparency that feels uncomfortable at first**

> Most of your communication will need to be written, and **people who aren't excellent written communicators may struggle on remote teams**.

### Decision Records

Help Scout has a transparent process for decision-making that includes a dedicated **#decision-records Slack channel** that lets everyone know:
- When a decision has been made
- What its impact is to the business
- Where to direct any follow-ups

## 6. Be Intentional About Relationship Building

In a remote culture, it's easier to be productive, but one of the tradeoffs is that **it's not as natural to develop a connection with your coworkers**.

### Things That Have Helped

1. **Fika** — A Swedish tradition of grabbing coffee with someone. Throwing a 15-30 minute fika on someone's calendar is a great way to put work aside and connect over shared interests.

2. **Luna Park** — A game engine that allows folks to connect for casual gaming.

3. **Annual company retreats and quarterly leadership off-sites** — In-person get-togethers where the team can meet and connect face to face.

4. **Employee Resource Groups (ERGs)** — Spaces (Slack channels, Zoom calls, sessions at retreat) where people can come together with others who share the same identities or lived experiences.

> **It's a lot harder being a manager in a remote culture than it is in a colocated one**, so Help Scout invests heavily in management training and support programs.

## 7. Create Clear Career Paths

When employees are in-office, it can be easier for them to spot opportunities for advancement and build relationships with mentors. In a remote culture, this may not happen organically.

### Two Distinct Paths

Help Scout has two distinct career paths:
1. **Manager**
2. **Individual Contributor (IC)**

> This may seem unusual as many people view climbing the ladder from IC to manager as the "goal," but **not everyone wants, or is suited, to manage**. And even if an engineer, designer, or support team member isn't a manager, they should still be able to progress in their career.

### Performance Review Cycles

Twice a year, planned opportunities for managers to work with direct reports to:
- Better understand which path they ultimately want to be on
- Help them create a game plan for how to get there

## 8. Optimize for Work-Life Harmony

Remote work skeptics often claim that people work less at home, but that hasn't been Help Scout's experience.

> The fact is, **when you hire people who love the work, some end up working straight through the day without even realizing it**. Of course, working non-stop isn't good either.

### Help Scout's Approach

- **PTO and holidays** allotment
- **Two week-long, company-wide breaks each year** — the entire business steps away
- **Paid sabbaticals every four years**
- **Time off for volunteering** in the community
- **Learning stipends** (and time off for learning)

> Remote workers need things pulling them away from work, like family, hobbies, and other passions, but along with that, **they need a company culture that supports and encourages those activities**.

### The Results

When you build work-life harmony into your culture, three great things happen:
1. Employees don't burn out
2. They do their best work
3. They're happy working at your company

## 9. Invest in Your People Team

Remote or not, the culture you build is a direct result of the time, effort, and resources you put into it.

> **One of the smartest things we've done as a company is invest in people ops.**

### Why Invest Early

Many of the issues faced by colocated companies at 50, 75, and 100 people actually have to be addressed in a remote culture at **10, 20, and 35**.

### What the People Team Does

- Creates benefits packages
- Manages the hiring and onboarding experience
- Leads DEI efforts
- Plans company retreats
- Runs training for managers and ICs
- Comes up with new ways to encourage engagement
- Collects feedback to know if the company is living up to employee expectations

> **A good culture doesn't just happen — you have to work at it!**

---

## Summary: Build a Remote-First Company That Lasts

To build a successful remote business, you need to foster a great remote culture that helps everyone feel **seen, respected, and fulfilled**.

> Good relationships, clear communication, and work-life balance — **these are all just ingredients of a good company culture, period**.
