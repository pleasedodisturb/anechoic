# Master Async Playbook

*Synthesized from 31 guides across 9 async-first companies: GitLab, Basecamp, Zapier, Doist, Automattic, Buffer, Gumroad, PostHog, Help Scout*

---

## Executive Summary

**The Core Insight (Doist):**
> Remote workers are more productive than their office-bound counterparts. But the benefit isn't location independence — it's **asynchronous communication** giving employees control over when they communicate.

**The Warning:**
> Teams who try to go remote without putting in place tools, workflows, and norms for asynchronous communication will fail.

---

## Part 1: Foundational Principles

### What is Async Communication?

| Synchronous | Asynchronous |
|-------------|--------------|
| Requires all team members to be present simultaneously | Communication happens with team members catching up on their own schedules |
| Everyone tied to same work schedule | Everyone works when it works best for them |
| Prioritizes **speed** of collaboration | Prioritizes **focus and flexibility** |

**Key Insight (Doist):** Async isn't primarily about tools — it's about **team culture and habits**. Even email is often treated as synchronous (average response time: 2 minutes).

### Why Sync-First Fails

**The Problem (Harvard Business Review):**
- Time spent on collaboration has increased **50%** over two decades
- Workers spend **80%** of their workdays communicating
- Email: 6 hours/day | Meetings: +253% since pandemic | Slack: 200+ messages/day

**The Four Costs of Real-Time Communication:**

1. **Constant Interruptions** → Can't do deep, cognitively-demanding work
2. **Connected Over Productive** → Incentivized to be online, not effective
3. **Unnecessary Stress** → Workers cope with "second shift" in evenings
4. **Lower Quality Decisions** → First response ≠ best response

> "Writing is thinking. To write well is to think clearly. That's why it's so hard." — David McCullough

## Part 2: The Async Communication Framework

### The Communication Stack (Best Practices)

| Company | Async % | Sync % | In-Person % |
|---------|---------|--------|-------------|
| **Doist** | 70% | 25% | 5% |
| **Gumroad** | ~95% | ~5% | 0% |
| **GitLab** | 80%+ | 15% | <5% |

### When to Use Sync vs Async

**Use ASYNC for:**
- ✅ Status updates and progress reports
- ✅ Documentation and decisions
- ✅ Feedback on written work
- ✅ Questions that don't need immediate answers
- ✅ Announcements and information sharing

**Use SYNC for:**
- 🤝 Building rapport (1:1s, team bonding)
- 💬 Critical feedback or sensitive topics
- 🧠 Brainstorming with many unknowns
- 🚀 Project kickoffs with many moving variables
- 🚨 Crises requiring immediate attention

> **"Synchronous communication should be the exception, not the rule."** — Doist

### The "Rule of Three" (GitLab)

> **When you go back and forth three times, jump on a synchronous video call.**

## Part 3: Writing-First Culture

### Overcommunication Formula (Doist)

| ❌ Under-communication | ✅ Over-communication |
|------------------------|----------------------|
| "Can you provide reporting once you get a chance?" | "Can you provide a short report (few paragraphs) on blog metrics from Google Analytics for **next Tuesday by 2pm EST**? I'd like to know: top posts, unique page views, bounce rate, and conversion rate. Thanks!" |

**Include:**
- 📋 What you need (specific deliverable)
- 📅 When you need it (exact deadline)
- 📊 What format (specifics)
- 🔗 Links to everything referenced

### The Handbook-First Approach (GitLab)

**The Problem:**
- Team members ask and re-ask for the same bits of data in perpetuity
- Creates a torturous loop of interruptions, meetings, and suboptimal knowledge transfers

**The Solution:**
1. **Document first, announce second** (not the other way around)
2. Create a **Single Source of Truth (SSOT)**
3. Make the handbook publicly accessible
4. **Limit chat retention** (GitLab keeps only 90 days of Slack)

> **"If the answer to a question isn't documented yet, immediately make a merge request to add it."** — GitLab

### What Goes in a Handbook?

**1. Company / Building Blocks**
- Mission, vision, values
- How we hire
- Product principles

**2. Groups/Departments**
- Org structure
- Team profiles
- Standard procedures

**3. The Day-to-Day**
- How we communicate
- Tools we use
- Policies

## Part 4: Meeting Culture Reform

### The Meeting Problem

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Presentations eat sync time | Pre-record on video, meetings are only Q&A |
| Meetings for brainstorming | Default to async thoughtful proposals first |
| No agenda | Every meeting has an agenda with edit rights |
| People late / no breaks | Use "Speedy Meetings" (25/50 min) |

### No-Meeting Days (PostHog)

> **"On Tuesdays and Thursdays, we don't have internal meetings at all."**

### Meeting Rules (GitLab)

1. **Start on time** — Do not wait for people
2. **End on time** — Respect everyone's next commitment
3. **Have an agenda** — Linked Google Doc, editable by all
4. **Take notes** — Everyone is responsible
5. **Record for async** — Those who couldn't attend can catch up
6. **Interrupting is OK** — Questions and context are valuable

### The Maker's Schedule (PostHog)

> **"If we have meetings at all, we'll cluster them around any stand-ups, so our day doesn't get split up."**

## Part 5: Transparency & Decision-Making

### Public by Default (GitLab, PostHog)

**What to make public:**
- Product roadmap
- Company handbook
- Decision discussions
- Meeting notes

**What stays internal:**
- Financial performance (until ready)
- Security vulnerabilities
- Personal HR information

### Decision Documentation (Help Scout)

**#decision-records Slack channel** that announces:
- When a decision was made
- What its impact is to the business
- Where to direct follow-ups

### RFCs - Requests for Comments (PostHog)

**When to use:**
- Large amount of work (2-3+ weeks)
- Introducing new technology
- Major feature or change
- Major customer impact

**Process:**
1. Identify problem and decision
2. Create RFC as pull request
3. Share and assign reviewers
4. Sync meeting if large disagreement
5. Document decision and merge

> **"You don't need to reach full agreement to decide, particularly if the decision is reversible."** — PostHog

## Part 6: Building Culture Without Co-location

### The Human Element Warning (Doist)

> At the beginning, Doist got rid of most meetings thinking they were a waste of time. They learned they had **lost the human element** — people felt disconnected.

> **"We are human after all"** — Daft Punk

### Connection Rituals

| Ritual | Company | Description |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **Fika** | Help Scout | 15-30 min coffee chats, Swedish tradition |
| **Red/Yellow/Green Check-ins** | Doist | Personal status sharing in team meetings |
| **Monthly Casual Hangouts** | Doist | Cross-team non-work video chats |
| **Pair Calls** | Zapier | Random coworker pairing for connection |
| **Weekly Team Meetings** | PostHog | Focused on rapport, not status updates |
| **Annual Retreats** | All | In-person company gatherings |

### Investment in People (Help Scout)

> **"Many issues faced by colocated companies at 50, 75, and 100 people have to be addressed in a remote culture at 10, 20, and 35."**

Help Scout invested in People Ops early for:
- Benefits packages
- Hiring and onboarding experience
- DEI efforts
- Company retreats
- Manager training
- Engagement measurement

## Part 7: Emergency & Escalation Protocols

### Emergency Channels (Doist)

> **"We use Telegram with notifications turned on for truly urgent incidents — a few times per year."**

**The 95/5 Rule:**
- 95% of communication is async (no urgency expectation)
- 5% has a dedicated urgent channel (notifications always on)

### Response Time Expectations

| Company | Expectation |
|---------|-------------|
| Doist | Within 24 hours |
| Gumroad | Within 24 hours |
| GitLab | Async = no urgency; 3x back-and-forth = video call |

## Part 8: Tools & Implementation

### The Async Tool Stack

| Purpose | Recommended Tools |
|---------|------------------|
| **Long-form Discussion** | GitHub Issues/PRs, Twist, Notion |
| **Documentation** | Handbook (Git-based), Notion, Confluence |
| **Quick Communication** | Slack (with 90-day retention!) |
| **Video Async** | Loom, pre-recorded presentations |
| **Video Sync** | Zoom, Google Meet, Around |
| **Emergency** | Telegram, SMS (notifications on) |

### Slack Best Practices (GitLab)

**DO:**
- ✅ Use public channels by default
- ✅ Use threads for all replies
- ✅ Use `:white_check_mark:` emoji when answered
- ✅ One message per topic (avoid multiple messages)

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Use DMs for work-related discussions
- ❌ Store decisions or official documents
- ❌ Use @here or @channel unless truly urgent
- ❌ Expect immediate responses

### Turn Off Notifications (Gumroad)

> **"Turn off all notifications from your phone!"**

Set specific time blocks to check and respond to messages instead.

## Part 10: Getting Started

### For Individuals

1. **Overcommunicate** — Include all context upfront
2. **Plan ahead** — "I need this in 2 days" not "ASAP"
3. **Say no to unnecessary meetings** — Suggest async alternatives
4. **Document after meetings** — Start a thread for those who weren't there
5. **Turn off notifications** — Batch message checking
6. **Check sharing settings** — Access delays kill async

### For Team Leaders

1. **Promote writing** as a core skill
2. **Evaluate on output**, not responsiveness
3. **Set response time expectations** (e.g., 24 hours)
4. **Use DRI model** — Single responsible person per area
5. **Make transparency a priority** — Open discussions by default
6. **Have emergency channels** — For true urgency only
7. **Invest in People Ops** — Earlier than you think

### Scripts for Declining Meetings (Dropbox)

> "Thanks for including me! I'm wondering if we could try to solve this in a thread instead?"

> "I've been in so many meetings lately, but I'm trying to be more disciplined about my schedule. Could we try to solve this without a meeting, first?"

> "I'd be happy to give you feedback on that! Before we schedule a meeting, could I review it in a Google Doc?"
